The moving discipline

Why slamming your legs into the ground moves the gut

The gut is not a fixed pipe — it's a soft, mobile organ that hangs in the abdomen and answers to mechanical force and gravity. When the methane has slowed everything to a crawl, one of the most reliable, drug-free ways to get it moving again is impact: the rhythmic, vertical jolt of footfall.

  • Impact agitates the contents. Each landing sends a shockwave through the trunk that physically jostles trapped gas and stagnant stool — loosening what a paralyzed muscle can't push, and helping it move toward the exit.
  • Movement wakes the sweeper. Brisk activity and the gastrocolic response stimulate peristalsis; this is the well-known "runner's gut" — the body's urge to empty once you start pounding the ground.
  • Real terrain beats a treadmill. The steep, uneven, volatile lanes of the Old City load the body in every plane — varied impact, inclines, descents, balance — turning a walk into a full-body, gut-shaking practice no flat machine can copy.
  • Gravity does part of the work. Upright, loaded, oscillating movement uses gravity to carry contents downhill through the sluggish bends — the same logic as the inversion work, applied on your feet.

How we run it & why the X-ray: impact is dosed and built up gradually, scaled or skipped on die-off days, and never forced. Because the work happens on hard, uneven ground — alongside inversions and the structural realities of a body that's been hunched and braced for years — a spine-integrity X-ray and your doctor's assessment are required before you come. Prepare yourself for volatile terrain; it is part of the medicine, and we make sure your frame can take it.

Framed as a working model and a widely-observed effect of movement on bowel transit — not a medical promise. Movement is dosed to you and to your clearance.

Fed by our kitchen

The carnivore table

Diet is roughly 90% of the work — so the kitchen is the centre of the house. Three carnivore meals a day, portioned and timed to starve the methane overgrowth of its fuel while rebuilding the gut lining.

  • Three meals, measured. About 100g at the first meal, 150g or more at the second, 150g at the third — enough to fuel and fat-adapt, never enough to overload a healing gut.
  • A tight window. Meals at roughly 08:00, 12:00 and 16:00 — last meal 16:00, nothing after 17:00. The fast does the overnight work.
  • Bone broth, daily, as medicine. Pressure-cooked for hours into collagen, glycine and minerals — sipped warm throughout.
  • Fatty, cooked meat & fish, salted heavily, always cooked through. The real electrolytes too: sodium plus potassium and magnesium.
  • Nothing that feeds the overgrowth. No sugar, grains, fermentable fibre, coffee or alcohol — a clean, zero-residue plate that lets the gut rest.
  • Careful reintroductions toward the end of the week — the method you take home, so you can widen your plate safely.
A note on the food

This is a therapeutic, time-boxed way of eating for the week — a demonstration and a kickstart, not a verdict that you'll eat this way forever. You leave knowing how to run this kitchen, and how to reintroduce foods, at home.

FAQ

Is there a medicine that cures SIBO/IMO?

No. There's no pill that cures methane-dominant SIBO/IMO. Antibiotics and supplements can help under a doctor, but lasting recovery is a complete change of daily habits — diet, fasting, movement, cold, breath, sleep and motility — practiced consistently. That's exactly what this week installs.

What does it cost and what's included?

₪10,000 per person, all-inclusive: 7 nights at The Way Inn, three carnivore meals daily, every session and instructor, medical supervision throughout, and all operators, fees and tax. Cohorts begin late September 2026.

What's the food and eating schedule?

Three carnivore meals daily — about 100g, then 150g+, then 150g — with bone broth, heavy salt, potassium and magnesium. Last meal at 16:00; no food after 17:00. No sugar, grains, fermentable fibre, coffee or alcohol.

What do I need to sign up?

A medical assessment by your own physician including a spine-integrity X-ray, plus written clearance and our health intake. Because the week includes impact/terrain movement, inversions and cold on the Old City's steep ground, we confirm your spine and health can handle it first.

Why impact and uneven terrain?

A stalled gut responds to mechanical force. Vertical impact and the steep, volatile lanes of the Old City rhythmically oscillate the abdomen, help dislodge trapped gas and stool, and stimulate peristalsis — turning a walk into a full-body, gut-shaking discipline. It's dosed and screened (hence the X-ray).

What are the cold and shower rules?

Ice immersion runs around 14°C — no colder needed. Hot showers stay short (4–5 min total), hot water never hits the belly, and every hot shower ends with at least one minute of cold. Cold is dosed, supervised, with a buddy, always optional.

Will I be cured in a week?

No — and anyone promising that is lying. A week is a launchpad: it gets you fat-adapted, installs the rhythm, and sends you home with a plan for your own doctor. The honest timeline is 90 days minimum, often 180 days to a year, with maintenance after.
